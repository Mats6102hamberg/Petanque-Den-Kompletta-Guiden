Appendix D

Building a Boule Court

Complete guide for your own court

A boule court of your own in the garden is the dream of many pétanque players. With proper planning and materials, you can create a playing surface that will last for many years and provide endless hours of playing enjoyment.

Planning and Preparation

Choose the Right Location

Before you start digging, think carefully about the placement:

Placement Checklist:

  • Size: At least 12 × 3 meters for a standard court (preferably 15 × 4 m if you have space)
  • Clear area around: At least 50 cm extra on each side for standing and moving
  • Slope: The ground should be relatively level – a slight slope (1-2%) for drainage is good
  • Sun/shade: Partial shade is ideal – full sun makes the court hot and dries it out quickly
  • Distance to house: Remember that boules bounce – keep distance from windows and delicate surfaces
  • Water drainage: Avoid low points where water collects

Dimensions and Measurements

Court Type Length Width Usage

Materials You Need

For a 15 × 4 Meter Court

Base Materials:

  • Landscape fabric (geotextile): 70-80 m² – prevents weeds and stabilizes
  • Base layer (crushed stone 0-32 mm): approx. 6-8 m³ (10-12 cm thickness)
  • Sub-base layer (stone dust 0-8 mm): approx. 3-4 m³ (5-6 cm thickness)
  • Surface layer (boule gravel 0-4 mm): approx. 2-3 m³ (3-5 cm thickness)

Edging Materials (optional but recommended):

  • Wooden border: Pressure-treated planks 50 × 150 mm, approx. 40 linear meters
  • Alternatives: Curbstone, concrete edging, or steel edging
  • Posts: 45 × 45 mm posts to secure the border, approx. 16-20 pieces

Tools:

  • Spade and excavator (or a lot of patience)
  • Spirit level and string line
  • Plate compactor (can be rented)
  • Rake and leveling tool
  • Wheelbarrow

Building the Court Step by Step

Step 1: Mark Out and Excavate

  1. Mark the outer edges of the court with stakes and string
  2. Check that the corners are square (measure the diagonals – they should be equal)
  3. Excavate 20-25 cm deep within the marked area
  4. Create a slight slope (1-2%) in one direction for water drainage
  5. Compact the bottom thoroughly with a plate compactor or hand tamper

Step 2: Lay Landscape Fabric

The landscape fabric is important to prevent weeds from growing through the gravel and to prevent the layers from mixing with the underlying soil.

  • Lay out the landscape fabric over the entire excavated area
  • Let the fabric extend up along the edges (it can be folded in later)
  • Overlap sheets by at least 20 cm

Step 3: Base Layer (Crushed Stone)

  1. Fill with 10-12 cm of crushed stone (0-32 mm)
  2. Spread evenly with a rake
  3. Compact thoroughly with a plate compactor – go over it several times
  4. Check that the surface is level and has the correct slope
  5. Water lightly and compact again for best results

Tip: This layer is the foundation – take time to do it properly. A good foundation creates a court that will last for many years.

Step 4: Sub-base Layer (Stone Dust)

  1. Add 5-6 cm of stone dust (0-8 mm)
  2. Spread evenly and level with a rake and straightedge
  3. Compact with a plate compactor
  4. Water and compact again
  5. Allow to settle for a few days before the next step

Step 5: Install the Border (if chosen)

Wooden Border:

  1. Drive posts along the edges of the court (approx. 1.5 m apart)
  2. Attach the planks to the inside of the posts
  3. Check that the border is level and vertical
  4. The border should extend approx. 5-8 cm above the finished surface

Alternative: Buried curbstones or steel edging give a cleaner appearance.

Step 6: Surface Layer (Boule Gravel)

  1. Add 3-5 cm of boule gravel (0-4 mm, preferably crusite limestone or granite gravel)
  2. Spread evenly with a rake
  3. Compact lightly – the surface layer should not become too hard
  4. Water gently and allow to settle
  5. Adjust any unevenness after a few days of play

Important: The surface layer should be firm enough to walk on but give slightly when the boule lands. Too hard = the boule bounces too much. Too soft = the boule "dies" and doesn't roll.

Choosing the Right Gravel

The Surface Layer is Crucial

The quality of play depends largely on the surface layer. Here are the most common options:

Gravel Type Properties Advantages Disadvantages
Crushed Limestone 0-4 mm Light colored, binds well Classic appearance, stable surface, binds with water Can become muddy in heavy rain
Granite Gravel 0-4 mm Gray/reddish, hard Very durable, drains well Doesn't bind as well, may roll more
Specialty Boule Gravel Optimized blend Perfect playing surface, professional feel More expensive, harder to find
Sand/Gravel Mix Softer surface Cheap, easy to work with Requires more maintenance, tracks easily

Recommendation

For most home courts, crushed limestone 0-4 mm is the best choice. It provides an authentic playing feel, binds well when watered, and is relatively inexpensive. Available at most building supply stores and gravel pits.

Court Maintenance

Regular Maintenance

After Each Game:

  • Level out holes and unevenness with a rake
  • Spread the gravel evenly across the surface

Every Week (with regular play):

  • Rake the entire court thoroughly
  • Water if dry – a moist surface plays better
  • Remove leaves and debris

Every Season:

  • Top up with new surface gravel where needed (normally 0.5-1 m³ per year)
  • Check the border for rot or damage
  • Clear any weeds along the edges

Troubleshooting

Problem Cause Solution
Standing water Poor drainage, low spot Add gravel, improve slope
Weeds growing through Landscape fabric missing/damaged Clear and apply weed killer, add gravel
Surface too hard Too much compaction Rake up and add new loose gravel
Surface too loose Too little compaction/too dry Water and compact lightly with roller or plate compactor
Gravel disappearing Wind, rain, play Normal – top up annually

Lighting and Accessories

Lighting for Evening Play

If you want to play in the evening, you need good lighting:

  • Brightness: At least 200-300 lux across the entire court
  • Placement: High (3-4 m) and to the side to avoid glare and shadows
  • Type: LED floodlights are energy-efficient and provide good light
  • Quantity: 2-4 floodlights depending on court length

Practical Accessories

  • Bench: Place one or two benches at the side for spectators and rest
  • Storage: A small box for boules, rake, and measuring tools
  • Scoreboard: Mounted at the side or freestanding
  • Sun protection: Parasol or pergola if the court is in full sun
  • Water hose: Practical for watering and rinsing the court

Summary

Building your own boule court is an affordable project that provides many years of playing enjoyment. The key success factors are:

  • Good planning: Choose the right location and size
  • Proper foundation: Take time with the base layer
  • Right surface material: Crushed limestone 0-4 mm is recommended
  • Regular maintenance: Rake and water for the best playing surface

Tips from Experienced Court Builders

"The best advice I can give is not to rush. Let each layer settle properly before moving on. A court built over a weekend will need a lot of adjustment in the first year. A court built over a month with proper compaction and settling between each step will play perfectly right away."
