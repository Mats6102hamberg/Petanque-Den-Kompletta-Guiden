Appendix A

Equipment Guide

Manufacturing, size, hardness and model selection for pétanque balls

Manufacturing

Pétanque is always played with metal balls. FIPJP's official rules require that balls are hollow and do not contain lead, sand, mercury or other foreign materials. They must also bear the manufacturer's name and weight and meet size and weight requirements.

Carbon Steel (acier carbon)

Advantages:

  • Porous surface, good grip: Carbon steel balls have a slightly rougher surface that provides better grip and more friction against the ground
  • Popular among pros: Professionals prefer carbon steel because the slight surface roughness gives better control

Disadvantages:

  • Rusts more easily: The porous surface makes them more sensitive to rust
  • Requires maintenance: Balls should be dried after play and oiled

Stainless Steel (inox)

Advantages:

  • Smoother surface: Gives less friction and makes it easier to release the ball quickly in shots
  • Almost maintenance-free: Stainless steel doesn't rust and requires less care
  • Perfect for beginners: Easy to keep clean, durable and maintains appearance

Disadvantages:

  • Gives slightly less feel in hand compared to carbon steel balls
  • Doesn't stop as easily on hard surfaces

Size and Weight

Official boules must have a diameter between 70.5 and 80 mm and weigh 650–800 grams. For children under 11 years, smaller balls of 65 mm and 600 g are allowed.

Lighter Balls (650–700 g)

  • Easier to control
  • Less weight makes it easier to throw softly
  • Gentler on hand and wrist
  • Suitable for beginners and players with less hand strength
  • Disadvantage: Less hitting power in shots

Heavier Balls (720–800 g)

  • More stable pointing – rolls less and stops faster
  • More power – gives more energy transfer in shots
  • Can move opponent's balls further
  • Disadvantage: Requires more strength and good technique

Diameter and Hand Size

Recommendations:

  • Pointers: Slightly smaller balls (71–73 mm)
  • Shooters: Slightly larger (73–76 mm) to reduce target profile
  • Standard for most: 73 mm and 680 g
  • Small-handed players: 70.5 mm

Hardness and Surface Texture

Ball "hardness" describes how much it bounces (elasticity) and how resistant it is to scratches. Manufacturers use different designations, but the most common categories are tendre (soft), demi-tendre (semi-soft) and dure (hard).

Hardness Category Behavior Use
Soft (tendre) Low bounce, absorbs collisions, "dies" quickly Pointing on asphalt, lob shots, carreau. Good control on hard surfaces.
Semi-soft (demi-tendre) Balanced bounce, good durability All-round play – both pointing and shooting. Suits most players.
Hard (dure) High bounce, rolls longer, very durable Pointing on soft surfaces (sand, gravel). Resistant to scratches.

Soft Balls

  • Low bounce: Absorbs collisions and "dies" quickly on hard ground
  • Ideal for shooters: Softness reduces rebound and increases chance of carreau
  • Disadvantage: Gets scratches more easily and wears faster

Hard Balls

  • High bounce: Rolls longer after landing
  • Very durable: Resists scratches and maintains appearance longer
  • Ideal for pointers on soft surfaces: Rolls well on sand and gravel
  • Disadvantage: Can bounce unpredictably on hard surfaces

Choosing the Right Balls

Pointing Balls

  • Lighter and smaller (680–700 g, 71–73 mm)
  • Soft or semi-soft hardness for better control
  • Smaller diameter gives better feel
  • Striated surface for better grip

Examples: Obut "RCC" (carbon steel, soft), La Boule Bleue "Compétition" (semi-soft), MS Pétanque "Classic" (tendre)

Shooting Balls

  • Lighter and softer (680–690 g)
  • Soft or very soft hardness minimizes bounce
  • Larger diameter (73–76 mm) gives better hitting surface
  • Smooth surface makes ball slip from hand

Examples: MS Pétanque "Black Spin" (very soft), La Boule Bleue "ProShooter" (soft), Obut "Match" (tendre)

All-round Balls

For players who both point and shoot, there are semi-soft balls (demi-tendre) that combine characteristics. Obut "Zen" (inox, semi-soft), JB Pétanque "Pro Series" (demi-tendre) and La Boule Noire "Competition" are popular all-round models.

Buying Advice – How to Choose the Right Balls

Beginners:

Start with an affordable set in stainless steel with medium hardness and weight around 700–720 g. Upgrade later when you know what you want to focus on.

Advanced Pointers:

Choose carbon steel balls between 680–710 g with soft–medium hardness for maximum feel.

Shooters (tireurs):

Choose soft balls around 680–690 g with larger diameter (73–76 mm) for better hitting surface and reduced bounce.

All-rounders:

Choose semi-soft balls around 700 g with medium diameter (73 mm) that work for both pointing and shooting.

Maintenance

After Each Game:

  1. Wash balls with warm water and dish soap
  2. Dry thoroughly with a clean cloth
  3. Oil lightly with maintenance spray or oiled cloth
  4. Store in dry environment in suitable bag

If Rust Occurs:

Scrub with steel brush or let sit in white wine vinegar, then rinse and dry. Oil again after cleaning.

Important: With regular maintenance, your boules will last for many years. Treat them well and they will serve you well on the court!
